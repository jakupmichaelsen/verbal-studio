# English B HF - Mad Men Oral Character Presentation

Assess an oral presentation/transcript about *Mad Men* season 1. The student should characterise four main characters and explain how they connect to the unit themes: USA in 1960, advertising, consumer culture, gender roles, identity, masculinity, femininity, marriage, work culture, and social expectations.

The presentation should last approximately 6 minutes and be delivered freely from notes, not read aloud.

## Requirements Checklist

- Covers Don Draper with characterisation and role in the series
- Covers Betty Draper with characterisation and role in the series
- Covers Peggy Olson with characterisation and role in the series
- Covers Pete Campbell with characterisation and role in the series
- Connects the characters to 1960s American society
- Connects the characters to advertising, consumer culture, identity, or gender roles
- Refers to relevant season 1 episodes, scenes, or concrete events
- Uses analytical terminology for fiction or TV drama
- Includes at least two texts from the Mad Men theme
- Uses the theme texts analytically, not only as named references
- Has a clear oral structure with introduction, transitions, and conclusion
- Sounds like a spoken presentation from notes, not a written essay read aloud
- Shows B2-level clarity, fluency, grammar, vocabulary, and analytical language

### Character Focus

Don Draper: successful advertising man, confident public image, hidden past, unstable identity, masculinity, power, emotional distance, appearance versus reality, and advertising as selling dreams.

Betty Draper: suburban housewife and mother, beauty and privilege, unhappiness, restriction, emotional isolation, domestic femininity, and critique of the ideal housewife image.

Peggy Olson: new secretary, outsider in a male-dominated office, ambitious and observant, challenges expected female roles, moves toward creative work, and represents change or female possibility.

Pete Campbell: young account executive, ambitious, insecure, status-conscious, privileged, entitled, contrasted with Don, and pressured by masculine success ideals.

### Analytical Terms To Reward

Characterisation, protagonist, contrast, conflict, setting, theme, symbolism, dialogue, body language, costume, props, lighting, camera angle, mise-en-scene, representation, stereotype, gender role, and social context.

### Analysis Levels

Level 1: Mostly descriptive, limited explanation, vague examples, limited terminology, weak connection to 1960s society, and missing or superficial theme texts.

Level 2: Explains characters and functions, uses some analytical terms, connects to themes such as gender roles or advertising, includes examples, and uses at least two theme texts with some relevance.

Level 3: Analyses character complexity, explains symbolic and thematic roles, connects scenes and historical context, uses at least two theme texts effectively, evaluates 1960s America, and has clear independent oral style.

### Grade Calibration

10-12 requires all four characters covered well, clear analysis, specific examples, strong thematic understanding, at least two theme texts used meaningfully, clear links to 1960s American society, and fluent spoken-style presentation.

7-10 is typical when all four characters are included, analysis is partly developed, some examples are used, theme texts are included but not deeply integrated, there is some thematic connection, and spoken style is mostly clear.

4-7 is typical when the presentation is mostly descriptive, examples are vague, terminology is limited, theme texts are missing or superficial, the unit connection is weak, or oral style sounds read aloud.

02-4 is typical when several characters are missing, there is little understanding of *Mad Men*, no relevant theme texts, very weak structure or language, or the presentation does not fulfil the task.

Do not treat missing episode references as automatically limiting the grade to 7 if the presentation otherwise shows strong task fulfilment. Around 10 can be appropriate when all four characters are covered clearly, the presentation explains what they represent in relation to 1960s America, at least two theme texts are used meaningfully, and the spoken structure and B2 language are clear.

Be careful with audio transcripts. If a conclusion or sentence appears odd, check surrounding lines before treating it as a content error. Do not penalise the student for a possible transcription mistake unless the transcript clearly supports that interpretation.
